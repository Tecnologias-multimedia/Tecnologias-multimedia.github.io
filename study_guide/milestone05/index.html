1 Description

Correlation is the term used in statistics for refering to the interdependency between random variables. It can be measured by the correlation coeﬃcient [1].

In the case of InterCom, the random variables are the two channels (left L and right R) of the stereo PCM signal [?]. In most cases, both channels are going to be highly correlated (especially if the microphone is mono), which means that we can represent one of them (for example, the R channel) with respect to the other (the L channel). From a mathematical point of view, this process can be seen as a decorrelation process. From a physical perspective, decorrelating implies energy accumulation [2].

To perform this inter-channel decorrelation, we use an orthogonal transform

y = Kx = 1 2 1 1 1 −1 x, (1)

where x represents a frame (a tuple or L and R samples, x[0] and x[1]), K is the 2 × 2 KLT (Karhunen-Loève Transform) matrix multiplied by 1∕2 (which is closely related with the Haar transform [4]), and y represents the transform coeﬃcients (in our case, a couple of coeﬃcients y[0] with the mean and y[1] with the diﬀerence). Notice that this transform is not orthonormal (energy preserving in the transform domain) because

∑ y[i]2 = 1 2∑ x[i]2, (2)

although both subbands y[0] and y[1] have the same gain (1∕2). This will simplify the quantization of y. The described transform is similar to the so called M/S stereo coding, but in our case, the división by 2 is carried on on the forward transform, instead of the backward (inverse) transform.

This transform can be implemented in-place using the following algorithm:

Algorithm 1.1: Inter-channel_decorrelation( )
procedure Analyze(frame) frame[0]− = frame[1] frame[1]+ = (frame[0] >> 1) frame[0] >>= 1 procedure Synthesize(frame) frame[0] <<= 1 frame[1]− = (frame[0] >> 1) frame[0]+ = frame[1]

where a− = b is a shorter representation of the operation a = a−b, and >> and << represent the bit-wise left and right shift operations, respectively. Notice that this type of in-place computations are commonly used in the implementation of DWTs (Dicrete Wavelet Transforms) using the Lifting Scheme [3].
