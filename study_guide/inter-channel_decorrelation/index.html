1 Description

InterCom transmit a stereo (left and right) signals. In most cases, both channels are highly correlated (especially when the microphone is mono), which means that we can represent one of them (for example, the right channel) with respect to the other (the left channel).

From a mathematical point of view, this process can be seen as a decorrelation process. From a physical perspective, decorrelating implies energy accumulation in a small number of coeﬃcients [4]. Correlation is a term used in statistics which refer to the interdependency between two random variables, and it can be measured by the correlation coeﬃcient [3]. In the case of InterCom, the random variables are the two channels (left and right) of the stereo PCM signal [1].

A major diﬀerence between transformed sequence y and the original sequence x is that the characteristics of the elements of the y sequence are determined by their position within the sequence [4].

To perform this inter-channel decorrelation we can use the transform

w = Kx = 1 2 1 1 −1 1 x, (1)

that is equivalent to

w[0] w[1] = 1 2 1 1 −1 1 x[0] x[1] ,

where x[0] and x[1] are the samples of the stereo frame x.

In Eq. 1 w represents the vector of transform coeﬃcients (in our case, a pair of coeﬃcients w[0] with the mean and w[1] with the diﬀerence of the samples divided by 2).

If the transform represented by the Eq. 1 can be also expressed by

w[u] = ∑ iKu,ix[i], (2)

where Ku,i denotes i-th element of the u-th row of the matrix K.

If the forward transform is performing the operations

w[0] = 1 2(x[0] + x[1]) w[1] = 1 2(x[1] − x[0]),

the inverse transform can be found by solving x[0] and x[1] in these equations, obtaining that

x[0] = w[0] − w[1] x[1] = w[0] + w[1],

that in matrix form becomes

x[0] x[1] = 1 −1 1 1 w[0] w[1] .

Notice that the inverse transform is the transpose of the forward transform. This is a characteristic of orthogonal transforms [4].

Orthonormal transforms are energy preserving [4].

Therefore, the inverse transform can be written as

x[i] = ∑ uKi,uw[u], (3)

and therefore,

x = KTw = K−1w = 1 −1 1 1 w. (4)

Notice that this transform (K) is not orthonormal (it is not energy preserving in the transform domain, i.e., the Parseval’s Theorem is not satisﬁed) because

∑ w[i]2 = 1 2(x[0] + x[1])2+1 2(x[1] + x[0])2 = 1 4(x[0]2+2x[0]x[1]+x[1]2)+1 4(x[0]2−2x[0]x[1]+x[1]2) = 1 2(x[0]2+x[1]2) = 1 2∑ x[i]2, (5)

but is orthogonal1 [4, 2], because the inner (also named dot) product of the basis vectors K1 = 1 1 and K2 = −1 1 is

⟨K1,K2⟩ = 1 1 ⋅ −1 1 = 0.

This means that K1 and K2 are linearly independent2 , and therefore they form a basis (set) [5]. This can be also veriﬁed with3

KKT = KTK = 2I, (6)

where I is the identity matrix.

The factor 2 in the Eq. 6 is indicating that the transform has a gain of 2 (the output of the inverse transform will be equal to the input to the forward transform multiplied by 2, which physically impled that we are amplifying the audio signal by 2). To avoid this, we can divide each coeﬃcient (w[0] and w[1]) by 2 after computing the forward transform.

Eq. 6 is showing also that the gain of coeﬃcient is the same: 2. This fact can be also veriﬁed comparing the energy of the reconstruction provided by each coeﬃcient:

1−1 1 1 1 0 = 11 = K1 1−1 1 1 0 1 = −11 = K2 11 2 := ⟨ 1 1 , 1 1 ⟩ = 1 1 ⋅ 1 1 = 2 −11 2 := ⟨ −1 1 , −1 1 ⟩ = −1 1 ⋅ −1 1 = 2,

where v2 denotes the L2 norm of the vector v.

This information, the gain of each coeﬃcient, is relevant for the quantization stage, which ideally, should allow to compress each pair of coeﬃcients with the same rate/distortion slope. To achieve this

Because both coeﬃcients have the same gain, the number of bits assigned by the quantizer should be the same

As it can be seen in this, the gain of each subband (and each coeﬃcient, because we have only one coeﬃcient per subband)

although both “subbands”4 w[0] and w[1] have the same gain (1 2, and therefore the same “importance” for a future quantization of w, i.e., the same quantization step can be used for the w[0] and w[1] coeﬃcients).

Notice that this procedure allows to ﬁnd the basis vectors (K1 and K2 in our case) from an implementation of the inverse tranform.5

The matrix K proposed is similar to the 2 × 2 KLT (Karhunen-Loève Transform), the Haar Transform [7] and the 2 × 2 Discrete Walsh-Hadamard Transform [4]). The described transform is also similar to the so called M/S stereo coding, but in our case, the división by 2 is carried on the forward transform, instead of the inverse (backward) transform. This is done to ensure that the transform can be computed in-place [6]. Such implementation is:

Algorithm 1.1: Inter-channel_decorrelation( )
procedure analyze(frame) frame[1]− = frame[0] frame[0]+ = (frame[1]∕2) frame[1]∕ = 2 procedure synthesize(frame) frame[1]∗ = 2 frame[0]− = (frame[1]∕2) frame[1]+ = frame[0]

where a OPER = b is a shorter representation of the operation a = a OPER b.
