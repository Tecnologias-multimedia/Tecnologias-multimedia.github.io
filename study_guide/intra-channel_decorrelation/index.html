1 Description

Correlation is a term used in statistics which refer to the interdependency between two random variables. It can be measured by the correlation coeﬃcient [3].

In the case of InterCom, the random variables are the two channels (left L and right R) of the stereo PCM signal [1]. In most cases, both channels are going to be highly correlated (especially if the microphone is mono), which means that we can represent one of them (for example, the R channel) with respect to the other (the L channel). From a mathematical point of view, this process can be seen as a decorrelation process. From a physical perspective, decorrelating implies energy accumulation in a small number of coeﬃcients [4].

To perform this inter-channel decorrelation, we can use an orthogonal1 transform [4, 2], that in the case of decorrelating a (digital) stereo frame x (a pair of L and R samples, x[0] and x[1]) is

w = Kx = 1 2 1 1 −1 1 x, (1)

that in equivalent to

w[0] w[1] = 1 2 1 1 −1 1 x[0] x[1] .

Here w represents the vector of transform coeﬃcients (in our case, a pair of coeﬃcients w[0] with the mean and w[1] with the diﬀerence of the samples).

The transform represented by the Eq. 1 can be also expressed by

w[u] = ∑ iKu,ix[i], (2)

where Ku,i denotes i-th element of the u-th row of the matrix K, that for our case performs the operations

w[0] = 1 2(x[0] + x[1]) w[1] = 1 2(x[1] − x[0]).

The inverse transform can be found by solving x[0] and x[1] in these previous equations, obtaining that

x[0] = w[0] − w[1] x[1] = w[0] + w[1],

that in matrix form becomes

x[0] x[1] = 1 −1 1 1 w[0] w[1] .

Therefore, the inverse transform can be written as

x[i] = ∑ uKu,iw[u], (3)

that in matrix form is

x = KTw = 1 −1 1 1 w. (4)

The matrix K proposed is similar to the 2 × 2 KLT (Karhunen-Loève Transform), the Haar Transform [7] and the 2 × 2 Discrete Walsh-Hadamard Transform [4]). The described transform is also similar to the so called M/S stereo coding, but in our case, the división by 2 is carried on the forward transform, instead of the inverse (backward) transform. This is done to ensure that the transform can be computed in-place.

Notice that this transform (K) is not orthonormal (it is not energy preserving in the transform domain, i.e., the Parseval’s Theorem is not satisﬁed) because

∑ w[i]2 = 1 2(x[0] + x[1])2+1 2(x[1] + x[0])2 = 1 4(x[0]2+2x[0]x[1]+x[1]2)+1 4(x[0]2−2x[0]x[1]+x[1]2) = 1 2(x[0]2+x[1]2) = 1 2∑ x[i]2, (5)

although both “subbands”2 w[0] and w[1] have the same gain (1 2, and therefore the same “importance” for a future quantization of w, i.e., the same quantization step can be used for the w[0] and w[1] coeﬃcients).

Notice that the (basis) vectors K1 = (1,1) and K2 = (1,−1) are linearly independent [5] (we cannot derive one from the other using the operations that deﬁne a vector space, or in other words, they inner product (also called dot product) is the zero vector (0,0)) and therefore, they form a basis (set).

This transform can be implemented in-place using the following algorithm:

Algorithm 1.1: Inter-channel_decorrelation( )
procedure analyze(frame) frame[1]− = frame[0] frame[0]+ = (frame[1]∕2) frame[1]∕ = 2 procedure synthesize(frame) frame[1]∗ = 2 frame[0]− = (frame[1]∕2) frame[1]+ = frame[0]

where a OPER = b is a shorter representation of the operation a = a OPER b. Notice that this type of in-place computations are commonly used in the implementation of DWTs (Dicrete Wavelet Transforms) using the Lifting Scheme [6].
