1 Description

1.1 Absolute Threshold of Human Hearing

Psychoacoustics has determined that the HAS (Human Auditory System) has a sensitivity that depends on the frequency of the sound, the so called THH (Threshold of Human Hearing). This basically means that some subbands can be quantized with a larger quantization step than others without a noticeable increase (from a perfection perspective) of the quantization noise.


Figure 1: Absolute threshold of human hearing.

A good approximation of the ToHH (Threshold of Human Hearing) for a 20-years old person can be obtained with [1]

T(f)[dB] = 3.64(f[kHz])−0.8 − 6.5ef[kHz]−3.3)2 + 10−3(f[kHz])4. (1)

This equation has been plotted in the Fig. 1.

1.2 DWT subbands and quantization steps

The number of DWT subbands

Nsb = Nlevels + 1 (2)

where Nlevels is the number of levels of the DWT. Except for the lNlevels subband, it holds that

W(hs) = 1 2W(hs−1), (3)

being W(⋅) the bandwidth of the corresponding subband s. Therefore, considering that the bandwidth of the audio signal is 22050 Hz, the bandwidth W(h1) of the h1 subband is 11025 Hz, W(h2) = 22025∕4, and so on. It also holds that

W(lNlevels) = W(hNlevels). (4)

The idea is to determine, knowning the frequencies represented in each DWT subband and the THH curve, the average quantization step that should be applied to each subband.

2 What you have to do?

  1. Given a Nlevels, determine the bandwidth of each subband. Using these bandwidths, ﬁnd the interval of frequencies of each subband. Using these intervals and the Eq. 1, determine a suitable quantization step Δs for each subband s, supposing that the subband with the highest sentibility (those that represents audio signals close to 3 KHz) should not be quantized (or quantized with a Δ = 1). Use the average of the function deﬁned by Eq. 1 in the corresponding subband s as Δs.
  2. Quantize and dequantize each subband with the corresponding Δs, using a dead-zone quantizer.
